Our Primary Route

Poland Type D Work Visa

The Type D is Poland's national long-stay visa for non-EU skilled workers. Unlike a Schengen tourist visa, it is specifically for employment and is issued for up to 3 years. The process starts before you arrive in Poland — the employer must first secure a work permit, which is then used to support your visa application at the Polish Embassy in your home country.

The 7-Step Process

1

Skills Verified

We confirm you hold the trade qualifications and experience that Polish employers require. If you have experience without a certificate, we guide you through the RPL process first.

2

European-Standard CV Built

We rebuild your CV to European format and produce a skills verification document. This is what licensed Polish agencies review before agreeing to represent you.

3

Agency Reviews Your Profile

Your CV and trade credentials are submitted to licensed Polish recruitment agencies actively hiring in your trade. The agency assesses your fit for current employer vacancies.

4

Employer Files Work Permit

Once matched, the Polish employer files your work permit with the relevant Voivodeship (regional labour authority). This takes 4–8 weeks depending on the region and trade category.

5

Work Permit Approval Received

The approved work permit letter is issued to the employer and shared with you. This document is the foundation of your visa application and must be included with your embassy submission.

6

Type D Visa Applied at Polish Embassy

We prepare your complete visa application pack — work permit letter, financial evidence, accommodation proof, insurance, cover letter — and you submit it at the Polish Embassy in your home country. Processing takes 2–4 weeks after submission.

7

Visa Issued — Travel to Poland

Your Type D national visa is collected from the Embassy. We provide a pre-departure briefing. You travel to Poland, begin work, and your visa also allows 90 days of Schengen travel across 27 countries.

Common Questions

Work Visa FAQ

Poland's labour market has strong demand for electricians, plumbers, HVAC engineers, welders, mechanics, automotive technicians, carpenters, and general construction workers. These trades are listed as shortage occupations, which means work permits are processed faster and employers are actively seeking candidates from outside the EU. If your trade is not listed here, ask us — the list is broader than most people expect.
A formal trade certificate is strongly preferred by Polish employers and agencies. However, hands-on experience without a certificate can still qualify through a Recognition of Prior Learning (RPL) process, where your skills are assessed and formally verified. This takes additional time but is a recognised pathway. We guide you through RPL as part of the European Work Preparation Package if needed.
No. Most skilled trade roles in Poland — particularly those filled through international recruitment agencies — are conducted in English at the management and documentation level. On the job site, basic communication is typically handled through bilingual supervisors. Polish language skills are an advantage for longer-term settlement but are not required at the entry stage.
Allow 3–5 months from the Work Readiness Assessment to visa issuance. The longest step is the work permit, which takes 4–8 weeks once the employer submits it to the Polish labour authority. After the work permit is issued, the Polish Embassy typically processes the Type D visa within 2–4 weeks. We advise starting as early as possible — the process cannot be meaningfully rushed once it is in the Polish government's hands.
The Polish government consulate fee for a Type D national visa is €80. This is paid directly to the Polish Embassy in your home country at the time of your visa appointment — it is not a GVJ fee and is not included in our service prices. GVJ fees cover the assessment, CV rebuild, agency matching, and visa document preparation. The €80 consulate fee is separate and non-refundable regardless of outcome.
Yes, dependants can apply for a family reunification visa once you are legally resident and employed in Poland. This is a separate process that begins after you are established there — it is not part of the initial work visa application. If family reunification is a priority for you, mention it at the assessment stage so we can factor the timeline into your planning.
